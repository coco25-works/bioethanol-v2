# Global Bio-Based Fuels Strategy Summary Report

**Last updated:** 2026-04-23
**Format:** 20-page briefing-style summary
**Audience:** Leadership, commercial / BD, compliance, operations, finance, and strategy teams
**Purpose:** Provide a complete but readable overview of the research pack so the team can align on priorities before going into the detailed deliverables.

## Page 1 - Executive Answer

The strongest near-term opportunity identified in this repository is:

**Waste-based HVO/FAME from Southeast Asia into Germany and the Netherlands.**

This answer is not based only on market size. It is based on the combination of regulatory demand, pathway reward, import practicality, buyer sophistication, and Southeast Asia feedstock fit.

The best routes are built around clearly defensible waste feedstocks, especially used cooking oil (`UCO`). Palm oil mill effluent (`POME`) and selected animal-fat or residue streams can also be attractive, but they need stronger classification discipline and buyer acceptance.

The central commercial lesson is simple:

**A biofuel route is attractive only when regulation becomes monetizable buyer pull.**

Many markets have policy ambition. Fewer markets create a compliance value that a Southeast Asia producer can actually access. Germany and the Netherlands currently do that better than the other markets reviewed.

The main recommendation is:

1. Build Europe-first around Germany and the Netherlands.
2. Use the UK as a useful second-wave or fallback market.
3. Treat the US federal market as valuable but approval-heavy.
4. Treat California, Japan, biomethanol, bio-LNG, and advanced ethanol as selective or monitor routes.
5. Avoid palm-based biodiesel into the EU and conventional bioethanol as the primary growth thesis.

The strategy only works if the company builds three capabilities:

1. Audit-ready waste documentation.
2. Recognized certification such as ISCC or RSB.
3. Contract structures that preserve producer value capture.

## Page 2 - What The Repository Is For

This repository is a structured decision-support system for bio-based fuels. It is meant to help a mixed team answer practical questions:

- Which regulations create demand?
- Which markets reward waste-based feedstocks?
- Which buyer classes can monetize the fuel?
- Which feedstocks can Southeast Asia supply competitively?
- Which proof systems are mandatory?
- Which markets should be pursued, monitored, or avoided?

The repository is organized in layers:

```text
Source registers
Country references
Pathway references
Market analysis
Strategy outputs
Appendices and QA
```

This matters because the strategy layer is only useful if it traces back to evidence. The repository therefore separates:

- legal and regulatory source logging
- jurisdiction explanation
- pathway explanation
- cross-market analysis
- commercial recommendations

The output is not a legal opinion. It is a strategy and diligence pack. Before signing contracts, entering a market, or making capital investments, legal counsel and current agency guidance should be checked.

## Page 3 - Core Strategic Conclusion

The repository supports a narrow, high-conviction conclusion:

**Southeast Asia should not try to sell every biofuel into every market. It should focus first on waste-based liquid fuels into the European markets where waste proof is rewarded and monetizable.**

The winning route has four parts:

| Component | Preferred answer |
|---|---|
| Feedstock | UCO first; selected POME, animal fat, and residue streams second |
| Pathway | HVO where possible; FAME where HVO is unavailable or less attractive |
| Market | Germany and Netherlands first |
| Proof | ISCC/RSB, waste classification, mass-balance discipline, audit-ready records |

This route wins because it combines supply-side fit and demand-side value:

- Southeast Asia has meaningful waste and residue streams.
- Europe has policy structures that reward waste and advanced feedstocks.
- Germany and the Netherlands have functioning compliance-value channels.
- Rotterdam provides logistics and trading advantages.
- Buyers in these markets already understand the premium logic.

The conclusion is not that other markets are irrelevant. The conclusion is that they should not consume scarce management attention before the first-wave route is built.

## Page 5 - Why Germany Leads

Germany is one of the strongest target markets because it converts regulatory obligation into visible commercial value.

The German logic is:

- obligated fuel suppliers need compliance value
- waste-based fuels can help meet compliance requirements
- THG-Quota value is commercially meaningful
- buyers and traders understand the credit logic
- importers can participate if proof and certification are strong

Germany is attractive for Southeast Asia because it does not only have demand. It has demand that can be monetized through a recognizable market structure.

The strongest Germany routes are:

- UCO to HVO
- UCO to FAME
- selected animal-fat or residue routes
- selected POME routes where classification is defensible

Main risks:

- weak waste proof
- THG value volatility
- buyer capturing too much of the compliance value
- tightening of waste classification rules

Germany should be treated as a first commercial target, but not as a low-documentation market. It is attractive precisely because the proof system matters.

## Page 6 - Why The Netherlands Leads

The Netherlands is almost as strong as Germany and has a different strategic advantage: Rotterdam.

The Dutch market matters because it combines:

- renewable transport fuel obligation
- HBE credit logic
- differentiated premium treatment
- trading and blending infrastructure
- maritime bunkering relevance

Rotterdam gives the Netherlands a practical edge. It can serve as:

- a European entry point
- a blending hub
- a trading hub
- a maritime-fuel gateway
- a relationship platform for counterparties

The strongest Dutch routes are:

- UCO to HVO
- UCO to FAME
- selected POME to HVO
- selected bio-LNG or maritime-linked routes as monitoring options

The Netherlands is not automatically better than Germany. Germany often has more visible THG value; the Netherlands has stronger logistics and hub advantages. Together, they form the core Europe-first strategy.

## Page 7 - Why The UK Is Priority 2

The UK is useful, but it does not outrank Germany or the Netherlands.

The UK remains attractive because:

- it has a functioning RTFO/RTFC system
- waste-based routes can be rewarded
- post-Brexit divergence can create flexibility
- English-language access is easier for many teams
- it can work as a second-wave or fallback market

The UK is not the top market because:

- scale is smaller than the EU core
- value pull is generally weaker than Germany/Netherlands
- it is less central to Southeast Asia logistics than Rotterdam

Use the UK when:

- Germany or Netherlands buyer access is delayed
- chain-of-custody flexibility matters
- a UK buyer offers better value capture
- the team wants a second market without the US approval burden

The UK should support the Europe-first strategy, not replace it.

## Page 8 - Why The US Is Selective

The US federal market is valuable but operationally heavier.

The RFS creates real demand through RINs. For qualifying waste-based fuels, the upside can be meaningful. The issue is access.

The US requires:

- EPA pathway approval
- Q-RIN readiness for foreign producers
- strong quality-assurance procedures
- ongoing reporting discipline
- importer and RIN-market sophistication

The US should not be treated as a fast-entry market.

It becomes attractive when the company is willing to fund an approval project and has a buyer or importer structure that does not absorb all upside.

Best use:

- second-wave expansion
- route diversification after Europe-first execution
- targeted pursuit where a US counterparty helps solve approval and compliance burden

The US is not weak. It is gated.

## Page 9 - Why California Is Not First-Wave

California has one of the most sophisticated low-carbon fuel systems in the world. That does not automatically make it the best market for Southeast Asia.

California rewards carbon intensity. Long-distance logistics can reduce the advantage of Southeast Asia supply because transport emissions affect the pathway score.

California is attractive where:

- the fuel has exceptionally strong CI performance
- the pathway can survive CARB approval
- the buyer can price the CI advantage
- logistics do not destroy the value

California should be treated as:

- selective for exceptional routes
- monitor for most Southeast Asia liquid-fuel exports

The main message is that California can like the fuel but still pay less than Europe if the route's CI score is weakened by distance.

## Page 10 - Why Japan Is Monitor

Japan is strategically relevant but commercially weaker as a first-wave destination.

Japan differs from Germany, the Netherlands, and the US because it is more policy-guided than credit-driven.

Key characteristics:

- bio-ETBE is the main product logic
- policy targets matter
- trading-house relationships matter
- import access can exist
- tradable compliance-value logic is weaker

Japan is not irrelevant. It is a relationship and positioning market, not a first-wave premium monetization market.

Current caveat:

- Japan remains translation-dependent for several primary and policy sources.
- The Fuel Quality Control Act has an official English translation for the Act No. 69 of 2014 version.
- Other Japanese legal and policy materials still need Japanese-language review.

Use Japan for:

- strategic monitoring
- niche bio-ETBE-linked channels
- long-term relationship development

Do not use Japan as the central near-term export thesis.

## Page 11 - Feedstock Logic

The best feedstock is not always the most abundant feedstock.

For premium export strategy, the feedstock must be:

- eligible
- defensible
- documentable
- auditable
- commercially available
- accepted by the buyer and certification scheme

Current feedstock hierarchy:

| Feedstock | Strategic reading |
|---|---|
| UCO | Cleanest first-wave waste route |
| POME | High potential, but classification-sensitive |
| Animal fats | Attractive where source and category are clear |
| Palm residues | Long-term advanced pathway potential |
| Cassava/sugarcane ethanol | Commodity benchmark, not flagship premium route |
| Palm oil | Avoid for EU liquid-fuel strategy |

UCO wins because it is already widely understood by buyers and certifiers.

POME is valuable but more sensitive because it is palm-linked. It needs careful proof that the feedstock is a true waste stream and not a disguised palm-oil route.

## Page 12 - Pathway Logic

The strongest pathways are waste-based HVO and FAME.

HVO is often preferred commercially because:

- it is a drop-in fuel
- it has strong buyer acceptance
- it can serve road and, indirectly, some maritime-related strategies
- it can capture waste-feedstock premium value

FAME remains relevant because:

- it is established
- it can be easier for some producers
- it can still work where buyers accept biodiesel blending
- it may be the realistic route before HVO capacity exists

Advanced ethanol is selective:

- it can gain value where feedstock is lignocellulosic or otherwise advanced
- it requires technology and investment
- it does not outrank waste-oil routes today

Biomethanol and bio-LNG are monitor routes:

- strategically relevant for maritime
- dependent on infrastructure and buyer uptake
- not first-wave for most Southeast Asia producers

## Page 13 - Certification And Chain Of Custody

Certification is not optional. It is the market-access gate.

The key systems are:

- ISCC
- RSB
- RED-recognized voluntary schemes
- market-specific reporting systems

The proof chain must show:

- feedstock origin
- waste classification
- collection records
- conversion records
- mass balance or accepted chain-of-custody model
- GHG calculation support
- audit trail

The common mistake is treating certification as the advantage. Certification is the ticket to enter. The advantage is high-quality waste access plus proof discipline.

Documentation failure can destroy the route even if the physical fuel is good.

The most important operating capability is therefore:

**audit-ready traceability from collection to shipment.**

## Page 14 - Buyer And Counterparty Logic

The buyer matters because the buyer often controls the compliance value.

Relevant counterparty types include:

- obligated fuel suppliers
- importers
- traders
- compliance-credit intermediaries
- bunker suppliers
- offtakers with registry access
- certification and verification providers

The producer should not only ask:

**Who will buy the fuel?**

The better question is:

**Who can monetize the fuel, and how much of that value can the producer retain?**

Contracting matters because a route can be strategically strong and still produce weak economics if the trader or buyer captures the full premium.

Preferred structures:

- indexed offtake where compliance value is partly shared
- direct sales to sophisticated obligated buyers where possible
- trader-led structures only when they solve access and share value fairly

## Page 15 - Supply Base Summary

Southeast Asia's strongest position is waste-based supply, not conventional commodity fuel.

Country context:

| Country | Main relevance |
|---|---|
| Indonesia | Palm supply, POME, UCO, B40 domestic absorption |
| Malaysia | Palm supply, POME, UCO, higher-blend mandate monitoring |
| Thailand | Ethanol benchmark, biodiesel context, B7/B20 policy monitoring |
| Singapore | Trading, bunkering, routing, hub value |
| China | Policy uncertainty and demand monitoring |
| Japan | Niche destination and policy-guided market |

The key regional supply point is:

**Southeast Asia can compete best when it exports certified waste-based value, not when it competes as a conventional commodity-fuel producer.**

This is why conventional ethanol is not the flagship route, even though Southeast Asia has ethanol production.

## Page 16 - Main Risks

The main risks are not only regulatory. They are operational and commercial.

| Risk | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Feedstock reclassification | Premium disappears if waste status is rejected |
| Documentation failure | Audit failure can block market access |
| Buyer value capture | Producer may carry burden but lose premium |
| Credit price volatility | Market value can fall after route setup |
| Palm-linked reputational risk | POME and palm residues need careful framing |
| RED III implementation divergence | Member-state rules can shift route economics |
| FuelEU methodology uncertainty | Maritime route modeling can change |
| US approval burden | Pathway approval can delay monetization |
| California CI penalty | Transport distance can weaken value |
| Japan translation dependency | Legal interpretation remains less certain |

The risk management answer is:

1. Build around defensible waste.
2. Document collection and conversion rigorously.
3. Use contracts that share compliance value.
4. Maintain active regulatory watchlists.

## Page 17 - No-Go Conditions

The team should stop or defer a route when:

- feedstock waste classification is uncertain
- certification cannot be obtained
- collection-level documentation is weak
- buyer captures all compliance value
- the route depends on palm oil into the EU
- California CI becomes uncompetitive after transport
- US pathway approval is not fundable
- maritime buyer uptake is speculative
- Japan route lacks a specific buyer relationship

No-go discipline matters because many biofuel routes look attractive at headline level. The hard work is identifying which routes are both eligible and commercially monetizable.

The repository's strongest value is not only identifying good routes. It is preventing the team from spending time on weak routes.

## Page 18 - What Each Team Should Do

Leadership:

- Approve Europe-first as the default strategy.
- Decide whether US approval investment is worth funding now or later.
- Require no-go checks before major management time is spent.

Commercial / BD:

- Map Germany and Netherlands buyers first.
- Separate true monetization counterparties from logistics-only counterparties.
- Negotiate value sharing explicitly.

Compliance:

- Build ISCC/RSB readiness.
- Standardize waste documentation.
- Maintain a source-gap and watchlist discipline.

Operations:

- Stress-test collection records.
- Confirm whether production can support chain-of-custody requirements.
- Prepare Rotterdam logistics scenarios.

Finance:

- Model direct sale, trader-led sale, and indexed offtake structures.
- Stress-test credit-price downside.
- Include documentation failure as a real commercial risk.

Monitoring is not a blocker. It is part of keeping the strategy current.

The highest-value update cycle is quarterly for Wave 1 markets and event-driven for major legal changes.

## Page 20 - Final Recommendation

The team can share this pack internally now.

The recommended message to the team is:

**We should focus first on certified waste-based HVO/FAME routes from Southeast Asia into Germany and the Netherlands.**

This is the strongest route because:

- demand is hard and compliance-driven
- waste-based feedstocks receive premium treatment
- buyers understand the value
- import access is practical
- Southeast Asia has relevant feedstock supply
- Rotterdam supports logistics and trading

The main execution priorities are:

1. Secure and document UCO and other defensible waste streams.
2. Build certification and audit readiness before aggressive selling.
3. Map Germany and Netherlands buyers and traders.
4. Structure contracts to preserve producer value capture.
5. Keep UK and US as second-wave options.
6. Monitor Japan, California, biomethanol, bio-LNG, and advanced ethanol without letting them distract from first-wave execution.

The pack is comprehensive enough for strategic discussion, explainable enough for mixed teams, and accurate enough for decision support with the caveats stated in the QA files.

Before binding commitments, obtain current legal/compliance review for the specific jurisdiction, pathway, feedstock, buyer, and contract structure.
