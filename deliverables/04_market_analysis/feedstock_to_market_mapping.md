# Feedstock-to-Market Mapping

**Last updated:** 2026-04-21  
**Primary audience:** Commercial / BD, operations, strategy, and supply teams  
**Decision supported:** Which Southeast Asia feedstocks should be routed to which pathways and destination markets

## What This File Helps You Decide
Use this file when the team needs to answer:

**Given the feedstock we actually control, what is the best route to market?**

This file is not trying to show every possible route. It is trying to show which routes are commercially worth serious attention.

## Executive Takeaway
The route logic in this repository is highly concentrated.

The strongest pattern is:
- **waste feedstocks**,
- converted into **premium liquid pathways**,
- sold into **Germany and the Netherlands**.

Everything else should be compared against that benchmark.

## Analytical Question
For each important Southeast Asia feedstock class, which pathway and destination combination is:
- most plausible,
- most monetizable,
- and most realistic to execute?

## Feedstock-to-Market Routing Matrix
Read the table below as a route-choice guide.

| SE Asia feedstock | Best pathway | Best destination | Why it works | Secondary routes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| UCO | HVO or FAME | Germany / Netherlands | strongest premium treatment and best market access | UK, US federal |
| POME | HVO | Germany / Netherlands | high potential if classification and proof are strong | UK, selected maritime-linked routes |
| Animal fats | HVO or FAME | Germany / Netherlands | premium treatment and strong route fit | UK, US federal |
| Palm residues | advanced ethanol | Germany / Netherlands | potential advanced-route premium | UK, US federal |
| Cassava | conventional ethanol | niche or lower-priority destinations | difficult to differentiate against incumbents | Japan niche fit |
| Conventional palm oil | FAME or HVO | limited and selective routes only | weak in Europe, politically exposed | highly selective non-EU cases only |
| POME-derived gas | bio-LNG / RNG | emerging maritime or selective transport-linked routes | strategically interesting but infrastructure-heavy | monitor rather than front-line push |

### What this table is really saying
The route logic is not evenly spread across feedstocks.

Some feedstocks are naturally aligned with premium markets. Others are not.

That means feedstock ownership is not just a supply issue. It is the starting point of strategy.

## Key Findings

### 1. UCO is the cleanest premium route in the repository
It is well understood, commercially accepted, and strongly rewarded across the best target markets.

### 2. POME is high-potential but more sensitive
POME can be strategically strong, especially for Southeast Asia, but classification and buyer acceptance need tighter handling than for UCO.

### 3. Animal fats are strong but narrower
They can be commercially attractive, but availability and collection scale may be more limited depending on origin market.

### 4. Conventional crop pathways are much weaker as flagship routes
Cassava and other conventional ethanol routes are not impossible. They are just harder to build a differentiated strategy around.

### 5. Conventional palm oil is the route to treat most cautiously
It is the weakest major feedstock class in the repository for compliance-focused export strategy.

### What this second table adds
The first table shows direction. This table shows quality.

It tells the team not just where a route can go, but whether it is worth management attention.

## What This Means For Different Teams

### For BD
Start with feedstocks that already fit premium market logic, not with feedstocks that merely exist in volume.

### For operations
Routes that look similar on paper may require very different proof, logistics, and customer handling.

### For strategy
The route map argues strongly for a waste-first portfolio, not a broad “all biofuels” thesis.

## What Non-Experts Usually Miss
- The best feedstock is not always the biggest feedstock.
- The route with the widest production base may still be a weak export strategy.
- Some routes fail because of politics and proof, not because of chemistry.

## Disqualifier Conditions
A feedstock-to-market route should be abandoned if:
- the feedstock classification is not defensible in the destination market
- the route requires conversion investment that the company cannot justify
- the destination market does not offer enough premium to cover logistics cost
- the route depends on politically exposed feedstock (palm oil into Europe)

## Worked Example: Why UCO Beats Cassava Even Though Cassava Is More Abundant
**Scenario:** A producer has access to large volumes of cassava and smaller volumes of UCO.

1. UCO is less abundant but qualifies for premium treatment in every major market.
2. Cassava is more abundant but faces commodity competition and cap pressure.
3. The smaller UCO route is commercially more attractive than the larger cassava route.
4. Feedstock volume does not equal strategic value.

### What this reveals
The strongest feedstock route is the one that unlocks premium treatment, not the one with the biggest supply volume.
