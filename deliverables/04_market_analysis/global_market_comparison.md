# Global Market Comparison

**Last updated:** 2026-04-23  
**Primary audience:** Commercial / BD, strategy, compliance, operations, and leadership teams  
**Decision supported:** Which global markets are most attractive for Southeast Asia-based biofuel exports, and why

## What This Comparison Is Trying To Answer
This file answers a practical question, not just an analytical one:

**If we are a Southeast Asia-based producer, which markets are actually worth spending time on first?**

That is different from asking which markets are largest, most ambitious, or most climate-forward on paper.

This comparison focuses on five things that matter commercially:
1. how strong the regulation-linked demand is,
2. whether the pathway is actually rewarded,
3. how hard the proof burden is,
4. whether imports can really participate,
5. and whether Southeast Asia supply has a realistic fit.

## Executive Takeaway
The result is straightforward:

- **Germany and the Netherlands are the most attractive first-wave markets** for Southeast Asia waste-based biofuel exports.
- **The US federal market is valuable but harder to enter** because the approval burden is real.
- **California is attractive in principle but structurally less friendly to long-distance Southeast Asia supply.**
- **The UK is a useful second-wave market.**
- **Japan matters strategically, but not as a top-priority commercial destination today.**

## How To Read The Ratings

| Rating | What it means |
|---|---|
| `Priority 1` | Strong enough to justify active commercial focus now |
| `Priority 2` | Attractive, but with a meaningful barrier or narrower value capture |
| `Selective` | Useful only in narrower conditions or for teams with a specific capability edge |
| `Monitor` | Strategically relevant, but do not prioritize now |
| `No-go` | Do not pursue under the current fact pattern |

## Market Comparison View
Read the table below as a decision screen. It is not saying which market is most important globally. It is saying which market is most usable for our supply base.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Germany | High | High (waste-based) | Medium | High | High | **Priority 1** |
| Netherlands | High | High (waste-based) | Medium | High (Rotterdam) | High | **Priority 1** |
| US federal | High | High | High (EPA approval) | Medium | Medium | **Priority 2** |

### What this table is really showing
Three markets stand out for different reasons:

- **Germany**: strongest mix of demand, value visibility, and market seriousness
- **Netherlands**: strongest logistics and routing advantage, plus premium treatment for the right pathways
- **US federal**: strong demand but much heavier access burden

This is why the table should not be read mechanically. A market can score well overall and still be a poor near-term choice if the entry barrier is too high.

## Key Findings In Plain English

### 1. Germany and the Netherlands lead because they combine premium treatment with practical access
These two markets are not simply large. They are useful. They reward the right feedstocks, have real buyer demand, and are accessible to importers who can prove sustainability and waste origin properly.

The strongest and cleanest version of this fit is usually `UCO` and other clearly defensible waste routes. Some other waste streams, especially `POME`, can also be attractive, but usually only as selective opportunities with stronger documentation and buyer acceptance.

### 2. The US federal market is strong but not easy
The US has a large and valuable compliance system, but foreign producers should not confuse that with easy access. The approval pathway is long enough and technical enough that it changes the commercial equation.

### 3. California is strong on paper but weaker for Southeast Asia in practice
California's carbon-intensity logic is sophisticated, but that sophistication cuts both ways. It rewards low-carbon fuels, but long shipping routes make Southeast Asia supply less competitive than it first appears.

### 4. France is not weak, but it is narrower than it looks
France has meaningful demand, but once you apply palm-related restrictions and look at the practical administrative burden, the window narrows considerably.

### 5. The UK is useful because it is flexible
The UK is not the largest or richest opportunity, but it is commercially usable and structurally helpful as a second-tier route.

### 6. Japan is strategically relevant but commercially weaker today
Japan matters for long-term positioning and for niche supply channels, but it does not currently compete with Europe on urgency or monetization structure. It is open enough to imports in principle, but it lacks the premium-paying demand structure that makes Europe-first routes stronger.

## Why The Leaders Lead

### Germany
Germany ranks highly because it combines:
- hard regulatory demand,
- a commercially meaningful premium mechanism,
- a buyer class that already knows how to transact,
- and a market structure where compliance value is visible rather than hidden.

For BD teams, that means it is easier to price, negotiate, and explain internally.

### Netherlands
The Netherlands ranks highly because it does two jobs at once:
- it is a compliance market,
- and it is a logistics and trading gateway.

That combination matters more than market size alone. Rotterdam does not just receive product. It helps route, blend, finance, and monetize product.

## Why Some Large Markets Still Rank Lower

### California
California looks strong because the `LCFS` is a respected low-carbon fuel system. But for Southeast Asia producers, long shipping distance adds carbon-intensity burden. In simple terms: California can admire the fuel and still pay less for it than Europe does.

### Japan
Japan is large as an economy, but it is not large as a premium-paying biofuel opportunity today. The missing ingredient is a strong tradable compliance-value mechanism.

### France
France is a serious market, but it narrows quickly when you remove palm-based routes and factor in heavier friction.

## Regulatory Asymmetry View
The list below highlights the small differences that matter commercially.

- **EU double counting vs US RINs**  
  The EU gives stronger relative upside to qualifying waste-based pathways than the US does for many comparable routes.

- **Netherlands HBE-A vs Germany THG-Quota**  
  The Dutch premium is more explicitly tiered; the German value is often more liquid and visible.

- **France palm treatment vs Germany / Netherlands**  
  France narrows the palm-based route far more aggressively.

- **Book-and-claim flexibility**  
  The Netherlands and UK are more flexible than Germany and France in how sustainability proof can be handled through the chain.

- **California CI penalty vs volumetric systems**  
  California penalizes distance in a way that the EU and US federal systems do not in the same way.

## Demand-Side Implications
- Transport mandates remain the central driver of demand.
- Maritime and aviation channels are becoming more important from 2025 onward.
- The markets that matter most are the ones that convert regulation into buyer urgency, not just policy aspiration.

## Supply-Side Implications
- Southeast Asia has meaningful waste-feedstock strength.
- Europe has stronger near-term routing logic than North America for that supply base.
- The US can still work, but only if the company is willing to treat access as a project in itself.

## Southeast Asia Producer Implications

### Priority 1 fit
- Waste-based biodiesel / HVO to Germany and the Netherlands

### Priority 2 fit
- Waste-based biodiesel / HVO to the UK

### Selective fit
- Waste-based routes to US federal market if pathway approval is achievable
- California for routes with unusually strong CI outcomes after transport

### Monitor
- Japan for relationship-driven niche channels
- France for palm-related routes

### What this second table adds
The first table shows who looks attractive. This second table shows **why**. For example:
- the US looks strong on demand but weaker on control,
- California looks strong on buyer pull but weaker on supply fit,
- and Japan looks easy on proof burden but weak on buyer urgency and value capture.

## Disqualifier Conditions
These are the issues that can kill a route even when the market looks attractive overall.

- Palm-based route into France
- No EPA pathway approval for US federal route
- Uncompetitive carbon-intensity score into California after transport is added
- Weak sustainability proof into any serious compliance market

## What Experts Should Pay Attention To
- where the premium is actually captured,
- whether the proof model is operationally manageable,
- whether logistics weaken an otherwise strong pathway,
- and whether a good market is only good for one narrow route rather than the broader portfolio.

## Worked Example: Why Germany Outranks Japan
**Scenario:** A Southeast Asia producer has waste-based HVO and can route it either to Germany or Japan.

1. **Germany** offers hard compliance demand, double-counting logic, and a market structure where the buyer has a clear reason to pay for the compliance value.
2. **Japan** offers softer policy interest and import openness in principle, but no equivalent tradable premium structure.
3. The same physical fuel is therefore usually worth materially more in Germany than in Japan.

### What this reveals
This is the central lesson of the whole comparison: **the best market is not the one with the most policy activity. It is the one where regulation becomes monetizable commercial pull.**
