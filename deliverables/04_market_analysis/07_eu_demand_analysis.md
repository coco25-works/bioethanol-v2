# EU Demand Analysis

**Last updated:** 2026-04-21
**Primary audience:** Commercial / BD team understanding EU demand dynamics in depth
**Decision supported:** Why the EU market looks the way it does for bio-based fuel demand

## Analytical Question
What creates the EU's demand structure and how do member-state implementations differ in practice?

### What This File Explains
The EU is not one market. It is a legal framework implemented through multiple member-state systems that produce very different commercial outcomes even when the fuel stays the same. This file explains why.

## Key Findings
- EU demand is driven by three parallel channels: RED transport targets, FuelEU Maritime, ReFuelEU Aviation
- Member-state implementation creates material asymmetries: Germany (THG-Quota credit market), Netherlands (HBE tiered premium), France (TIRUERT volumetric)
- Waste-based pathways are structurally advantaged across all member states through double counting
- Import infrastructure through Rotterdam is well-established
- RED III tightens conventional caps and raises targets — increasing demand for waste-based and advanced pathways

## Demand Drivers
1. **RED III transport target**: 29% RES in transport by 2030 — member states implement through national systems
2. **FuelEU Maritime**: 2% GHG intensity reduction from 2025 — ships at EU ports
3. **ReFuelEU Aviation**: 2% SAF blending from 2025 — at EU airports
4. **EU ETS**: Carbon pricing for aviation and maritime — increases compliance cost of fossil fuels

## Member-State Demand Differences

| Dimension | Germany | Netherlands | France |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary mechanism | THG-Quota | HBE | TIRUERT |
| Credit/premium liquidity | High | Medium | Low |
| Waste-based premium | High | High | Medium-High |
| Import infrastructure | Good (Hamburg) | Best (Rotterdam) | Good (Le Havre, Marseille) |
| Bunkering access | Limited | Strong | Limited |
| Administrative friction | Medium | Medium | Higher |

## Disqualifier Conditions
An EU demand route should be reconsidered if:
- the route does not qualify for premium treatment in the target member state
- the administrative friction is too high relative to expected value
- the palm-related feedstock is targeted at France (structurally weak)

## Worked Example: Why Germany's Demand Design Rewards Waste-Based Routes More
**Scenario:** Two member states (Germany and France) both have demand. A producer targets both with waste-based HVO.

1. Germany's THG-Quota creates visible, tradable compliance value for waste-based routes.
2. France's TIRUERT creates volume-based demand but with less differentiated premium treatment.
3. The same fuel route generates more commercially visible value in Germany.
4. The demand mechanism design determines the route economics, not just the market size.

### What this reveals
Within the EU, demand mechanism design differences can change route economics as much as market size differences.
