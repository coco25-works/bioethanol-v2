## What This File Helps You Decide
Use this file when the team needs to answer:

**What proof do we need to access the market, and where can a strong fuel still fail because the documentation is weak?**

## Executive Takeaway
Across this repository, the most important recurring commercial truth is:

**A strong pathway without strong documentation is not a strong pathway.**

Certification is not the value driver. It is the access gate. If the gate fails, the premium usually fails with it.

## Why This Topic Matters
The difference between a premium cargo and a discounted cargo is often not the molecule. It is the proof.

That is especially true for:
- waste-based routes,
- advanced pathways,
- and any route where the premium depends on feedstock classification.

## How To Read This File
This file is not just a list of accepted schemes. It is a guide to where market access breaks in practice.

## In Plain English: What Certification Actually Does
Certification:
- proves the feedstock and fuel meet market sustainability rules
- helps the buyer trust that the route qualifies for premium treatment
- gives the market a basis for audit and enforcement

Certification does **not**:
- guarantee premium capture on its own
- replace feedstock-level documentation
- eliminate route-specific approvals

## Cross-Market Recognition Overview

| Proof or recognition mechanism | EU core markets | US federal | California | UK | Japan |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| `ISCC` | Commonly accepted and commercially central | Not the main legal gate | Not the main legal gate | Commonly useful in practice | Useful credibility signal |
| `RSB` | Accepted in key contexts | Not the main legal gate | Not the main legal gate | Useful in practice | Useful credibility signal |
| EPA pathway approval | Not relevant | Core access gate | Not relevant | Not relevant | Not relevant |
| CARB pathway approval | Not relevant | Not relevant | Core access gate | Not relevant | Not relevant |

## Chain-of-Custody Model Differences

| Model | Where it is strongest | Commercial meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Mass balance | Core EU practice | Most standard route for serious compliance trade |
| Book-and-claim | More flexible in the Netherlands and UK | Can make some supply chains easier to execute |
| Identity preserved | Strongest where the buyer wants maximum traceability | Highest confidence, often more operationally demanding |

## Market-Specific Proof Requirements

### EU and EU-linked compliance markets
- sustainability certification
- feedstock-origin proof
- waste-versus-conventional classification
- GHG calculation
- chain-of-custody integrity

### US federal market
- pathway approval
- regulatory registration
- route qualification
- foreign-producer quality assurance where relevant

### California
- pathway approval
- carbon-intensity treatment
- lifecycle methodology
- route-specific reporting discipline

### UK
- recognized sustainability proof
- route qualification under RTFO logic
- practical chain-of-custody handling

## Recurring Access Barriers
The five failures that most often destroy value are:
1. certification exists, but feedstock proof underneath it is weak
2. the wrong proof system is being used for the destination market
3. the chain-of-custody model is too rigid or too loose for the destination
4. the GHG or route-calculation method is wrong for the market
5. the commercial team assumes certification means value capture is solved

## What This Means For Southeast Asia Producers

### Baseline rule
If the company wants access to serious compliance markets, it should treat certification and documentation capability as core commercial infrastructure, not support functions.

### Practical priority order
1. build strong sustainability certification capability for the main target markets
2. build feedstock-level documentation discipline
3. build chain-of-custody management that fits destination-market expectations
4. build route-specific approval capability where the market requires it

## Worked Example: How A Strong Cargo Loses Its Premium
**Scenario:** A producer ships UCO-derived fuel with accepted top-level certification, but collection-level records are incomplete.

1. The route appears strong at the summary level.
2. During audit, the buyer or regulator questions the waste-origin evidence.
3. The waste-based treatment is challenged.
4. The premium collapses, even though the physical fuel itself has not changed.

### What this reveals
Certification is necessary, but it is not enough on its own. **Documentary integrity at the collection and chain level is what protects premium value.**

## What Non-Experts Usually Miss
- certification does not equal automatic premium capture
- the destination market may care more about route approval or carbon methodology than about the certification brand itself
- a sophisticated market can reject a poorly documented route faster than it rejects a technically average but well-proven one

## What Experts Should Pay Attention To
- differences between recognition and actual market acceptance
- differences between scheme-level acceptance and route-level defensibility
- where the proof burden is likely to tighten over time because of fraud or enforcement pressure
