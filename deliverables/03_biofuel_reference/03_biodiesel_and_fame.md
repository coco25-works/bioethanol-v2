# Biodiesel and FAME — Cross-Market Pathway Reference

**Last updated:** 2026-04-21  
**Primary audience:** Commercial / BD, compliance, operations, finance, and strategy teams  
**Decision supported:** Where biodiesel / FAME demand is strongest, how treatment differs by market, and when the route is commercially attractive for Southeast Asia-based supply

## What This File Helps You Decide
Use this file when the team needs to answer:

**When is biodiesel / FAME a strong commercial route, and when is it only technically eligible but strategically weak?**

That distinction matters because biodiesel is not rewarded equally across markets. The fuel chemistry can stay the same while the economics change dramatically depending on feedstock origin, waste classification, and proof quality.

## Executive Takeaway
Biodiesel / `FAME` remains a very important pathway, but it is no longer a simple “sell wherever there is mandate demand” product.

The strongest version of the route is:
- **waste-based FAME**,
- backed by strong sustainability proof,
- sold into markets that visibly reward waste-based feedstocks.

The weakest version of the route is:
- **conventional crop-based FAME**, especially palm-based,
- sold into markets where caps, ILUC pressure, or lower premium treatment make it hard to monetize.

In practical terms: **UCO-based FAME is commercially attractive; palm-based FAME is increasingly a problem route.**

## Why This Pathway Matters
`FAME` is still one of the most established and widely traded liquid biofuel pathways in the world. It matters because:
- it is already commercially understood,
- it can often be produced at lower capital cost than HVO,
- and it is still rewarded meaningfully in several compliance systems.

But the route has become much more selective.

For a mixed team, the key point is simple:

**the commercial outcome depends less on the word “biodiesel” and more on what feedstock produced it, how it is documented, and which market receives it.**

## How To Read This File
This file is built around one commercial question:

**Does the destination market reward this FAME route enough to justify selling it there?**

To answer that, the reader should focus on four things:
1. whether the feedstock is waste-based or conventional,
2. whether the route receives premium regulatory treatment,
3. whether proof of origin is strong enough,
4. whether the market still has enough practical value after restrictions and competition are applied.

## Feedstock and Conversion-Route Scope

### In scope
- FAME from `UCO`
- FAME from animal fats / tallow
- FAME from conventional vegetable oils such as rapeseed, soybean, sunflower, and palm
- FAME from some palm-related waste streams where classification is accepted

### Out of scope
- `HVO` / renewable diesel (covered separately)
- straight vegetable oil routes
- SAF-specific conversion routes

### What matters most here
The key boundary is **waste-based vs conventional**.

That is the commercial dividing line for FAME in almost every serious target market.

## What FAME Really Is In Commercial Terms
For non-specialists, it helps to think of FAME as the more established, more common, and often lower-capex cousin of HVO.

Its advantages:
- mature production route,
- broad market familiarity,
- lower capital intensity than HVO.

Its disadvantages:
- blending limits matter more than for HVO,
- it is more exposed to “conventional biofuel” caps,
- and lower-quality feedstock classifications can drag its economics down quickly.

## Regulatory Demand by Market
Read this table as a commercial-value guide, not just a legal-treatment summary.

| Market | Demand mechanism | FAME treatment | Practical value level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Germany | THG-Quote | Strong for waste-based FAME | High |
| Netherlands | HBE system | Strong for waste-based FAME, especially premium category treatment | High |
| France | TIRUERT | Eligible, but much narrower for palm-related routes | Medium |
| US federal | RFS | Can generate strong value if the route qualifies correctly | High |
| California | LCFS | Can work, but transport distance weakens economics | Medium-High |
| UK | RTFO | Stronger for waste-based / development-fuel routes | Medium-High |
| Japan | Policy-guided market | Limited direct relevance for FAME | Low |

### What this table is really saying
FAME still works best in markets that do one of two things:
- explicitly reward waste-based routes, or
- make compliance value visible enough that the route can still be priced properly.

That is why Germany and the Netherlands remain strong, while Japan does not.

## Eligibility and Restrictions by Market

| Market | Waste-based FAME | Conventional FAME | Palm-based FAME |
|---|---|---|---|
| EU-level logic | Strong treatment | Weaker, capped | Increasingly pressured |
| Germany | Strong | Weaker | Restricted / commercially weakened |
| Netherlands | Strong | Weaker | Restricted / commercially weakened |
| France | Stronger if non-palm and well-proven | Weaker | Materially disadvantaged |
| US federal | Can still be viable if approved | Can still qualify, but category value matters | Depends on pathway approval and economics |
| California | Possible, but CI-sensitive | Possible, but often weaker | Possible in theory, weaker in practice |
| UK | Stronger for waste-derived routes | Weaker | Less attractive |

### What non-experts usually miss
“Eligible” and “attractive” are not the same thing.

FAME can still be legally eligible in a market and yet be commercially weak because:
- it sits in a lower-value treatment category,
- it is exposed to cap pressure,
- or it loses ground to HVO and better-scoring alternatives.

## Certification and Traceability Requirements
The pathway does not work commercially unless the proof works.

Typical requirements:
- sustainability certification such as `ISCC` or `RSB` in the main compliance markets,
- feedstock-origin proof,
- chain-of-custody integrity,
- GHG documentation,
- and, in some markets, route-specific regulatory approval.

### In plain English
For FAME, feedstock paperwork is often almost as important as the fuel itself.

That is especially true for waste-based value claims.

## Route Competitiveness and Proof Burden
The route economics are highly uneven.

### Strongest FAME routes
- `UCO -> FAME`
- `animal fat -> FAME`
- some clearly accepted waste-based routes with defensible proof

### Weaker FAME routes
- conventional crop-based routes,
- especially where they face caps,
- and especially where palm-related scrutiny is high.

### Why the proof burden matters
Waste-based FAME often looks more attractive on paper than it is in practice if the documentation stack is weak. Fraud sensitivity around used oil and some palm-adjacent waste streams means the route should be treated as documentation-intensive, not just production-intensive.

## Cross-Market Asymmetry Map

| Asymmetry | Markets compared | Why it matters commercially |
|---|---|---|
| Waste premium visibility | Germany vs Netherlands | Germany gives better liquidity visibility; Netherlands gives clearer tiered premium logic |
| Palm-route pressure | France vs Germany / Netherlands | France is materially less friendly to palm-related FAME routes |
| Distance penalty | California vs most volumetric systems | Long-distance Southeast Asia supply is weakened by CI treatment |
| Route flexibility | UK / Netherlands vs stricter continental practice | Some markets are easier to operationalize for imported FAME |
| FAME vs HVO preference | All major markets | HVO often has structural advantages, even where FAME is still viable |

### What experts should pay attention to
The key asymmetry is not only between countries. It is also between **route versions of the same fuel**. Waste-based FAME and conventional FAME increasingly behave like different commercial products.

## Southeast Asia Supply Implications
FAME is still relevant to Southeast Asia, but selectively.

### Best fit
- `UCO`-based FAME
- animal-fat-based FAME where available
- some waste-based palm-side streams if classification is robust and buyer acceptance exists

### More sensitive fit
- `POME`-linked liquid routes where market acceptance depends on stronger proof and classification confidence

### Weak fit
- conventional palm-based FAME into Europe
- conventional ethanol-style “commodity logic” applied to FAME

## Worked Example: Why Two FAME Cargoes Can Have Totally Different Value
**Scenario:** A Southeast Asia producer has two FAME cargoes:
- one made from `UCO`,
- one made from conventional palm oil.

1. The UCO cargo enters a market like Germany and improves the buyer's compliance position meaningfully.
2. The palm-based cargo may still be technically possible in some places, but it carries more regulatory pressure, weaker premium treatment, and worse long-term defensibility.
3. The result is that the two cargoes may look similar physically but behave like different commercial products.

### What this reveals
For FAME, **feedstock identity is strategy**.

## What This Means For Our Team
- Treat waste-based FAME as a serious route, especially where HVO capacity is constrained or a lower-capex option is needed.
- Do not treat all FAME as one category in internal planning.
- Keep `UCO` as the cleanest and most scalable premium route.
- Treat palm-based FAME into Europe as a structurally weak route unless something very specific and unusual changes.
