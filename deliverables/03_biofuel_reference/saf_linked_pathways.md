# SAF-Linked Pathways - Cross-Market Reference

**Last updated:** 2026-04-23  
**Primary audience:** Commercial / BD team assessing SAF relevance for covered pathways  
**Decision supported:** Where aviation demand changes the relevance of biofuel pathways and what SAF-specific treatment applies

## What This File Helps You Decide
- whether a pathway you are already considering has a viable SAF upgrade route
- which SAF conversion pathway is most relevant given your feedstock and geography
- where SAF mandates create hard demand that changes the investment case
- what additional ASTM, sustainability, and traceability requirements apply

## Executive Takeaway
SAF is not a separate industry. It is a demand layer on top of existing biofuel pathways.

For Southeast Asia producers, the most relevant SAF route is HEFA from waste oils because it uses the same waste-based supply chains that already matter for HVO. Alcohol-to-Jet is a secondary route that needs additional conversion investment. FT-SPK from biomass is longer term. POME-linked SAF should be treated as a conditional route, not a default premium route, because the same residue-acceptance uncertainty follows through into SAF.

## Why This Pathway Matters
SAF demand is growing rapidly because aviation now has hard mandates and strong policy incentives in key markets. That demand can pull value into waste oils, bioethanol, and biomass routes that would otherwise be judged only on road-fuel economics.

## SAF Conversion Routes and Feedstock Connections

| SAF route | Feedstock connection | Key markets | Maturity |
|---|---|---|---|
| HEFA | Waste oils, animal fats, selected residues | EU, US, UK | Commercial |
| AtJ | Bioethanol, isobutanol | EU, US | Early commercial |
| FT-SPK | Biomass gasification, waste | EU, US | Demonstration / limited |
| e-SAF | Green hydrogen plus CO2 | EU | Pre-commercial |

## Regulatory Demand by Market

### EU
ReFuelEU creates the strongest hard SAF demand signal. That makes the EU the most important SAF destination market in this repository.

### UK
The UK SAF mandate is smaller than the EU in absolute scale, but commercially relevant and directionally aligned.

### US
The US relies more on incentives than mandates. SAF demand can still be real, but the pull is less structurally clear than ReFuelEU.

### CORSIA
CORSIA matters as a background demand layer, not as the strongest near-term premium source.

## Certification and Traceability Requirements
- ASTM approval is a technical fuel-quality gate
- sustainability certification is a market-access gate
- waste-derived SAF inherits the same waste-proof burden as waste-derived road fuel
- documentary failure at the waste-collection level can destroy both the waste premium and the SAF premium

## Route Competitiveness and Proof Burden

| SAF route | Feedstock availability in SE Asia | Proof burden | Competitiveness |
|---|---|---|---|
| HEFA from UCO | High | Medium | strongest near-term route |
| HEFA from POME | High | High | conditional, not default |
| AtJ from bioethanol | Medium | Medium | viable but capex-sensitive |
| FT-SPK from biomass | Medium | Medium-High | long-term only |

## Cross-Market Asymmetry Map
- the EU provides the strongest mandate-backed pull
- the UK is directionally similar but smaller
- the US can still be attractive, but economics depend more on incentive structure
- waste-derived SAF can capture unusually high value where waste treatment and SAF value stack together

## Southeast Asia Supply Implications

### HEFA from waste oils - the near-term play
- strongest fit with current Southeast Asia waste-based supply chains
- best aligned with the current HVO opportunity set
- most practical way to access SAF demand in the next few years

### AtJ from bioethanol - the mid-term option
- depends on having both ethanol supply and conversion capability
- commercially relevant, but more build-intensive

### FT-SPK from biomass - the long-term option
- strategically interesting for palm residues and other biomass
- high capex remains the main constraint

## Worked Example: UCO-to-HEFA-SAF for EU Demand
**Scenario:** A fuel supplier needs qualifying SAF for an EU airport and sources HEFA-SAF produced from Southeast Asia UCO.

1. The SAF demand exists because the mandate is real, not voluntary.
2. The route works because the waste-oil proof chain is already defensible.
3. The same UCO becomes more valuable because it feeds aviation demand rather than road-only demand.
4. If the waste proof fails, the route can lose a meaningful part of its value even if the physical fuel remains usable.

### What this reveals
SAF raises the value of strong waste-based pathways. It does not rescue weak proof chains.

## What Non-Experts Usually Miss
- SAF is a demand layer, not a fully separate technology universe
- HEFA is the only broadly proven near-term SAF route that strongly overlaps with current Southeast Asia strengths
- POME-linked SAF should be treated as conditional until the residue-proof case is accepted by real counterparties

## What This Means for Our Team
- prioritize HEFA-linked waste-oil routes when SAF relevance matters
- treat AtJ as a build-first option, not a first-wave route
- treat POME-linked SAF as selective and proof-heavy
- use EU SAF demand as the main benchmark for commercial attractiveness
