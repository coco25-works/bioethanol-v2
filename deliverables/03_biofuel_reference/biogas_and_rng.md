# Biogas and RNG — Cross-Market Pathway Reference

**Last updated:** 2026-04-21  
**Primary audience:** Commercial / BD, compliance, operations, finance, and strategy teams  
**Decision supported:** Where biogas / RNG creates real transport-fuel value, why end use matters so much, and when the route is attractive for Southeast Asia

## What This File Helps You Decide
Use this file when the team needs to answer:

**Is biogas / RNG a serious export route, or mainly a high-value route with difficult logistics?**

The answer is both: the route can be very valuable, but it is much harder to move and monetize than the leading liquid-fuel pathways.

## Executive Takeaway
Biogas / RNG can create very strong value in some markets, especially where transport use receives premium treatment.

But for Southeast Asia producers, the route is constrained by one simple reality:

**gaseous value is often easier to create than to move.**

That makes biogas / RNG highly interesting in theory, but more selective in export strategy than HVO or biodiesel.

## Why This Pathway Matters
Biogas / RNG matters because it can sit in some of the strongest premium categories in major compliance systems, especially when the feedstock is clearly waste-based.

The route becomes commercially compelling when:
- the gas is upgraded,
- the end use is transport,
- and the market recognizes it as a premium low-carbon route.

## How To Read This File
The biggest commercial question here is not feedstock alone. It is **end use**.

That is because the same gas can have very different value depending on whether it is used for:
- transport,
- heat,
- power,
- or maritime fuel.

## Feedstock and Conversion-Route Scope

### In scope
- biogas from manure, landfill gas, food waste, agricultural residues, and `POME`
- upgraded `RNG` / biomethane for transport use
- bio-LNG where relevant to transport and maritime logic

### Key distinction
The route only becomes strategically attractive for this program when it reaches a transport-linked use case. That is where the strongest premium logic usually appears.

## What Biogas / RNG Really Is In Commercial Terms
Biogas / RNG is one of the clearest examples of a pathway that can be very strong on paper but hard to execute commercially across borders.

Why:
- the credit value can be very high,
- but the logistics are much more difficult than for liquids.

## Regulatory Demand by Market

| Market | Main demand logic | Practical relevance for RNG |
|---|---|---|
| Germany | THG-Quote and transport-use treatment | High |
| Netherlands | HBE-G and transport-related logic | Medium-High |
| California | Very strong low-CI route in the right cases | Very High |
| US federal | Strong for qualifying cellulosic / transport routes | Very High |
| UK | Real but smaller than US / California upside | Medium |
| EU maritime context | Emerging through bio-LNG / maritime routes | Emerging |

### What this table is really saying
Biogas / RNG can be one of the highest-value pathways in the repository, but only in the right market and the right end-use channel.

## Eligibility and Restrictions by Market
- transport use is the key value trigger in most serious markets,
- waste-based gas routes are much stronger than generic gas routes,
- maritime-linked routes are emerging but not yet central.

## Certification and Traceability Requirements
As with other premium waste-based pathways, the route depends on:
- feedstock proof,
- route documentation,
- chain-of-custody integrity,
- and end-use defensibility.

## Route Competitiveness and Proof Burden
The biggest weakness in this pathway is not usually regulation. It is logistics.

### Why the route can be strong
- premium treatment can be excellent,
- some low-carbon scoring outcomes can be extremely attractive,
- and waste-based gas routes are strategically well aligned with decarbonization trends.

### Why it can still underperform in practice
- moving gas is harder than moving liquids,
- exportability is weaker,
- and commercial execution often requires infrastructure that many producers do not yet control.

## Southeast Asia Supply Implications
For Southeast Asia, the most relevant gas-side route is usually tied to waste streams such as `POME`.

That creates strategic interest, but not necessarily immediate exportability.

### Best use cases
- domestic or regional transport use where infrastructure exists,
- future bio-LNG / maritime routes if infrastructure and buyer pull improve,
- selective long-term development rather than near-term front-line export focus.

## Worked Example: Why A High-Value Route Can Still Be Hard To Scale
**Scenario:** A producer has access to strong POME-derived gas potential.

1. The route may be very attractive in carbon terms.
2. The market may reward transport use strongly.
3. But the route still struggles if the producer cannot move, upgrade, and deliver the gas economically.

### What this reveals
Biogas / RNG is a route where **infrastructure discipline matters as much as policy upside**.

## What Non-Experts Usually Miss
- This can be a high-value pathway and still not be a first-wave export route.
- The end use matters more here than in most liquid-fuel pathways.
- The route often makes more sense as a build-first or monitor route than as a short-term flagship opportunity.

## What This Means For Our Team
- Treat biogas / RNG as strategically important but operationally constrained.
- Do not put it ahead of waste-based HVO / biodiesel for near-term export focus.
- Keep particular attention on `POME`-linked gas routes where domestic, regional, or maritime use may become more viable over time.
