# EU-Level Regulatory Framework

**Last updated:** 2026-04-21
**Primary audience:** Commercial / BD team screening EU biofuel opportunities
**Decision supported:** How the EU regulatory framework creates demand for bio-based fuels and what asymmetries exist across member states

## Why This Market Matters
The European Union is the largest regulated demand center for bio-based fuels globally. Unlike voluntary or incentive-based systems, the EU creates compliance-driven demand through binding renewable energy targets, mandatory GHG intensity reductions in maritime transport, and SAF blending obligations at airports. These mechanisms pull bio-based molecules into the market regardless of oil price or consumer preference. The critical complication is that member-state implementation creates significant asymmetries: the same fuel pathway can capture very different value depending on which member state receives it, how the compliance system is designed, and which actor retains the credit.

## Market Overview
The EU biofuel market is driven by three parallel demand channels: transport fuels under RED II/III, maritime fuels under FuelEU Maritime, and aviation fuels under ReFuelEU Aviation. Each channel uses a different compliance design — volumetric targets with GHG weighting for transport, GHG intensity reduction for maritime, and SAF blending percentages for aviation. The transport channel is the largest and most mature, with the THG-Quota (Germany), HBE system (Netherlands), and TIRUERT (France) creating distinct member-state markets that reward different pathways, feedstocks, and proof models.

## In-Scope Fuel Pathways
- Biodiesel and FAME (fatty acid methyl ester)
- Renewable diesel and HVO (hydrotreated vegetable oil)
- Bioethanol
- Biomethanol (emerging under maritime rules)
- Biogas and renewable natural gas (RNG)
- Sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) — relevant through ReFuelEU Aviation

## Governing Regulatory Framework
- **RED II** (Directive (EU) 2018/2001): Core directive establishing 2030 renewable energy targets, sustainability criteria, GHG methodology (Annex V), and feedstock categorization (Annex IX). Article 25-30 governs biofuel sustainability.
- **RED III** (Directive (EU) 2023/2413): Amends RED II, raising the transport RES target to 29% by 2030 (or 14.5% sub-target for advanced biofuels). Tightens Annex IX feedstock caps. Transposition deadline: May 2025.
- **FuelEU Maritime** (Regulation (EU) 2023/1805): GHG intensity obligation for ships at EU ports starting 2025. 2% intensity reduction in 2025, scaling to 80% by 2050. Creates maritime biofuel demand.
- **ReFuelEU Aviation** (Regulation (EU) 2023/2405): SAF blending obligation at EU airports starting 2025 (2%), scaling to 70% by 2050. Creates aviation biofuel demand.
- **EU ETS** (Directive 2003/87/EC): Carbon pricing for aviation (since 2012, full surrender 2026) and maritime (from 2024, phase-in 2024-2026).
- **Delegated Regulation (EU) 2019/807**: Defines Annex IX Part A and Part B feedstock lists. Critical for double counting and waste-based pathway economics.

## Eligible Pathways
Pathways that clearly count toward EU compliance:
- Biodiesel from waste oils and residues (Annex IX Part A) — double counting
- HVO from waste feedstocks — double counting
- Bioethanol from conventional crops (subject to cap)
- Bioethanol from lignocellulosic feedstocks (Annex IX Part A) — double counting
- Biogas/RNG from waste sources — double counting
- SAF from HEFA, Fischer-Tropsch, AtJ, and other approved pathways under ReFuelEU

## Restricted / Capped / Conditional Pathways
- **Conventional biofuels cap**: RED III caps conventional biofuels (from food/feed crops) at 2021 member-state level or 7% of transport energy, whichever is lower.
- **Annex IX Part B feedstocks**: Count toward targets but do NOT receive double counting. Include conventional crops, palm oil (with ILUC risk classification), and others.
- **High-ILUC-risk feedstocks**: Palm oil-based biofuels face phase-out under RED III (2030: no high-ILUC biofuels counted). This is a de facto restriction on palm-oil biodiesel.
- **Crop-based biofuels**: Subject to the 7% cap. Market increasingly shifting toward waste-based pathways.

## Demand Mechanism
Three parallel mechanisms create demand:
1. **Transport RED targets**: Member states must achieve a 29% renewable energy share in transport by 2030 (RED III). Each member state implements through national systems (THG-Quota, HBE, TIRUERT, etc.).
2. **FuelEU Maritime**: Ships at EU ports must reduce fuel GHG intensity by 2% (2025) scaling to 80% (2050). Pooling and banking of compliance surplus allowed. Non-compliance incurs penalties.
3. **ReFuelEU Aviation**: Fuel suppliers at EU airports must blend SAF at increasing percentages (2% 2025 → 70% 2050). Non-compliance incurs penalties.

## Obligated Parties / Buyer Classes
- **Transport**: Fuel suppliers (gasoline, diesel) obligated under national implementations of RED. They must demonstrate renewable energy content or surrender credits.
- **Maritime**: Ships calling at EU ports are obligated under FuelEU Maritime. Ship operators must reduce GHG intensity.
- **Aviation**: Fuel suppliers at EU airports are obligated under ReFuelEU Aviation. They must blend specified SAF percentages.

## Buyer / Counterparty Classes
- Obligated fuel suppliers (Mineralölunternehmen, fuel distributors)
- Refiners and blenders integrating biofuels into fossil fuel supply
- Biofuel traders and aggregators
- THG/HBE/RTFC certificate traders
- Maritime fuel suppliers (bunker fuel suppliers at Rotterdam, Hamburg, etc.)
- Aviation fuel suppliers (into-plane fueling companies)
- Compliance-value intermediaries

## Certification and Chain-of-Custody Requirements
EU compliance requires sustainability certification under recognized voluntary schemes:
- **ISCC** (International Sustainability and Carbon Certification) — most widely used
- **RSB** (Roundtable on Sustainable Biomaterials)
- **REDcert** and **REDcert²**
- Other Commission-approved schemes

Chain-of-custody models: mass balance is the primary model accepted under EU rules. Book-and-claim is accepted in some member states (e.g., Netherlands, UK) but not uniformly across all EU implementations. This creates an asymmetry: the Netherlands' acceptance of book-and-claim for HBE credit purposes gives it more flexible sourcing than France's more restrictive approach.

## Import / Trade / Local-Preference Considerations
- Imported biofuels can access EU compliance markets if they meet RED sustainability and GHG criteria.
- No formal local-content preference in RED, but member-state implementation can create de facto import barriers through registry access, verification requirements, and administrative friction.
- High-ILUC-risk feedstock phase-out affects palm-oil-based imports primarily from Indonesia and Malaysia.
- Sustainability certification must be verified at the point of origin by approved certifying bodies.

## Temporal Rules and Transition Windows
- **RED III transposition deadline**: May 2025 — member states must update national laws
- **FuelEU Maritime**: 2% GHG intensity reduction from 2025
- **ReFuelEU Aviation**: 2% SAF blending from 2025
- **EU ETS maritime**: phase-in 2024-2026 (40% 2024, 70% 2025, 100% 2026)
- **Annex IX Part A phase-in**: progressive counting treatment changes
- **High-ILUC biofuel phase-out**: complete by 2030
- **Counting multipliers**: RED III introduces multipliers for renewable electricity in transport, RFNBOs, and advanced biofuels — creating time-limited premium windows

## Implementation and Administrative Friction
- Member states transpose RED into national law at different speeds and with different interpretations
- Registry access varies: Germany's BAFA, Netherlands' RVO, France's DGEC each have different registration requirements
- Certification scheme approval is EU-level (Commission), but local implementation and audit burden varies
- Importer treatment differs: some member states treat importers more favorably than others for compliance credit purposes
- Documentary failure risk: loss of sustainability proof can collapse value retroactively

## Supply-Side Context
- EU domestic biofuel production is substantial but insufficient to meet targets
- Germany and France are the largest domestic producers
- Waste-based pathways are supply-constrained domestically — creates import opportunity
- Palm oil from SE Asia faces regulatory headwinds (ILUC classification)
- UCO (used cooking oil) is the most demanded feedstock but supply is limited globally

## Southeast Asia Relevance
The EU is a realistic target market for SE Asia-based producers, but with significant pathway filters:
- Waste-based biodiesel and HVO from SE Asia waste oils (UCO, POME) are the strongest fit — Annex IX Part A treatment and double counting
- Bioethanol from conventional crops is capped and faces competition from domestic EU production
- Palm oil-based biodiesel faces ILUC restrictions and potential phase-out
- Biogas/RNG from SE Asia waste streams is theoretically eligible but faces chain-of-custody and transport complexity
- SE Asia producers need EU-recognized certification (ISCC, RSB) to access the market

## Value-Capture Mechanics
The actor who captures compliance value varies by member-state implementation:
- **Germany**: THG credit value typically accrues to the obligated supplier who can trade certificates; producers can capture value by selling directly with THG credit attached
- **Netherlands**: HBE premium structure creates differentiated value — HBE-A (waste-based) premium captured by whoever holds the HBE unit
- **France**: TIRUERT value captured primarily by obligated fuel suppliers; producer value capture depends on contract structure
- General pattern: value capture favors actors with direct access to the compliance registry and obligated-party contracts

## Priority Legal Asymmetries for Cross-Market Comparison
1. **Germany vs Netherlands**: THG-Quota credit vs HBE premium — different value structure, different market liquidity, different registry access requirements
2. **Germany vs France**: THG-Quota vs TIRUERT — different counting rules, different treatment of waste feedstocks, different administrative friction
3. **EU vs UK**: Post-Brexit divergence — separate certification, development fuel category in UK, different obligation design
4. **EU vs US federal**: Volumetric with GHG weighting (EU) vs pure volumetric (US RFS) — creates different pathway economics
5. **EU vs California**: GHG-intensity-based at member-state level (various) vs GHG-intensity-based at state level (LCFS) — similar concept, different implementation details
6. **Book-and-claim acceptance**: Netherlands accepts it; Germany and France are more restrictive — changes source flexibility

## Commercial Decision Criteria
A BD team should assess the EU market based on:
- Pathway waste feedstock qualification (Annex IX Part A = double counting = highest value)
- Certification readiness (ISCC or RSB certification is table stakes)
- Which member state to target (Germany for credit liquidity, Netherlands for bunkering access, France for scale)
- Import documentation integrity (sustainability proof must survive audit)
- Value-capture contract structure (producer vs trader vs obligated supplier)

## What Would Change This Conclusion
- RED III implementation delays or divergent member-state interpretations
- Tightening of Annex IX Part A feedstock definitions
- Expansion or contraction of high-ILUC classification
- New member-state compliance designs (e.g., if France moves to a different system)
- FuelEU Maritime or ReFuelEU Aviation implementation delays

## Worked Example: Waste-Based Biodiesel Under Annex IX Part A
**Scenario**: A SE Asia-based producer exports UCO-derived biodiesel (FAME) to a German fuel supplier.

1. **Classification**: The UCO-derived biodiesel qualifies under Annex IX Part A — double counting toward the transport target.
2. **THG credit**: Under Germany's BEHG, the fuel generates a THG credit based on its GHG intensity versus the fossil fuel baseline.
3. **Double counting effect**: Because it's Annex IX Part A, the volume counts twice toward the obligation — effectively doubling its compliance value per liter.
4. **Value capture**: The value of the THG credit depends on the contract structure. If sold FOB with sustainability certificate attached, the producer captures some value. If sold CIF Rotterdam for further trading, the trader may capture the premium.
5. **What this reveals**: Annex IX Part A treatment is the single most important pathway economics factor in the EU. Feedstock qualification determines whether a pathway gets this treatment.
