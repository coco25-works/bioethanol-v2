# Thailand — Biofuel Market Reference

**Last updated:** 2026-04-23  
**Primary audience:** Commercial / BD, supply, and strategy teams  
**Decision supported:** How Thailand affects regional ethanol and biodiesel competition and what role it plays in Southeast Asia planning

## What This File Helps You Decide
Use this file when the team needs to answer:

**Is Thailand a target market, or mainly a regional competitor and benchmark?**

The answer is: **mainly a regional benchmark and competitor**.

Thailand is not a first-wave export destination, but it is one of the most important markets for understanding how Southeast Asia's biofuel landscape develops — especially for ethanol.

## Executive Takeaway
Thailand matters because it is the largest ethanol producer in Southeast Asia and has established blending mandates for both ethanol and biodiesel. For biodiesel, B7 remains the standard reference point and B20 exists in policy tracking; B10 sales should not be treated as current after the 2024 discontinuation.

It is not one of the main export targets in this repository, but it is essential for understanding regional cost position, feedstock competition, and how Southeast Asia evolves as a biofuel supply base.

## Why This Market Matters
Thailand matters because:
- it has the most mature ethanol market in Southeast Asia,
- sugarcane and cassava-based ethanol production creates a regional cost benchmark,
- domestic biodiesel mandates compete with Malaysia and Indonesia for palm-derived feedstocks,
- and its Alternative Energy Development Plan (AEDP) sets a long-term policy direction that affects regional dynamics.

## How The Market Actually Works
Thailand uses blending mandates to drive biofuel use in transport fuel. Unlike Germany's GHG-performance system, Thailand operates on a volumetric mandate model — fuel distributors must blend specified percentages of ethanol and biodiesel.

### In plain English
Thailand is where a lot of Southeast Asia's ethanol gets made and used. If you are thinking about ethanol anywhere in the region, you need to understand what Thailand is doing — because it sets the price floor and competitive baseline.

### Compared with Malaysia
- Malaysia is palm-focused (biodiesel only).
- Thailand has both ethanol and biodiesel mandates with diversified feedstocks (sugarcane, cassava, palm).
- Thailand's ethanol production is the largest in Southeast Asia.

### Compared with Indonesia
- Indonesia is palm-biodiesel dominant with no significant ethanol market.
- Thailand is the regional ethanol leader.
- Both have biodiesel mandates, but with different feedstock bases.

## Market Overview
Thailand is most useful in this repository as:
- a regional benchmark for conventional ethanol economics,
- a domestic-demand reference market for understanding Southeast Asia blending dynamics,
- and a context market for assessing feedstock competition (especially sugarcane and cassava).

## In-Scope Fuel Pathways
- bioethanol (sugarcane-based, cassava-based)
- biodiesel (palm-based)
- selected regional low-carbon transport-fuel context

## Governing Regulatory Framework
- **Alternative Energy Development Plan (AEDP) 2018-2037**: the core policy framework setting biofuel targets and blending mandates; AEDP 2024 update under development
- **Energy Conservation Act B.E. 2535 (1992, amended)**: general energy conservation legal foundation
- **EPPO E10/E20 ethanol mandate**: E10 is the standard blend; E20 and E85 available at pumps
- **EPPO biodiesel mandate**: B7 is the standard reference point; B20 exists in policy tracking; B10 sales were discontinued in 2024
- **TIS fuel quality standards**: technical specifications for blended fuels

### What this means in practice
Thailand's regulatory system is straightforward — volumetric mandates enforced through fuel distributors. There is no GHG-value premium mechanism. The market rewards volume compliance, not differentiated environmental performance.

For a team targeting European premium markets, Thailand's relevance is competitive and benchmarking rather than as a destination.

## Eligible Pathways
Thailand's mandate system covers:
- sugarcane-based ethanol (the primary feedstock)
- cassava-based ethanol (significant secondary feedstock)
- palm-based biodiesel (for B7 and B20 policy context)
- no formal policy support for waste-based or advanced pathways

## Restricted / Capped / Conditional Pathways
- crop-based ethanol is the dominant pathway — there is no formal restriction, but also no policy incentive for waste-based routes
- palm-based biodiesel faces feedstock competition from Malaysian and Indonesian domestic mandates
- B20 operational details still need monitoring because they affect domestic palm demand
- no formal caps in the EU-sense, but the system does not differentiate between pathway classes

### What non-specialists usually miss
Thailand's biofuel system is built around agricultural commodity support (sugarcane, cassava, palm oil) — not around GHG performance or environmental premium. This means Thai biofuel pricing reflects agricultural economics, not compliance value.

## Demand Mechanism
Demand is created by volumetric blending mandates:
- **E10**: 10% ethanol in gasoline (standard, mandatory)
- **E20**: 20% ethanol blend (available, promoted)
- **B7**: 7% biodiesel in diesel (standard reference point)
- **B20**: recognized in policy tracking and relevant to palm demand
- **B10**: discontinued in 2024; do not treat as a current demand channel

### Why buyers care
Fuel distributors must meet the blending ratio. Feedstock source is not differentiated in the way European systems distinguish between waste-based and crop-based. The buyer needs liters of ethanol or biodiesel — not compliance value.

## Obligated Parties / Buyer Classes
Fuel distributors placing gasoline and diesel on the Thai market are the obligated parties, administered through EPPO.

## Buyer / Counterparty Classes
- major Thai fuel distributors (PTT, Bangchak, Thai Oil)
- ethanol producers supplying the domestic mandate (Mitr Phol, Thai Roong Ruang, and others)
- palm biodiesel producers
- agricultural cooperatives connected to sugarcane and cassava supply chains

## Certification and Chain-of-Custody Requirements
- Thailand does not have a mandatory sustainability certification system comparable to EU ISCC or RSPO
- The system focuses on fuel quality standards (TIS) rather than sustainability proof
- For domestic compliance, no chain-of-custody certification is required
- For export to European markets, ISCC or RSPO certification would be needed — this is a separate, additional requirement

### Why this matters commercially
Thai biofuel production is certified to Thai fuel quality standards, not to international sustainability frameworks. Any export route to Europe requires building a parallel certification infrastructure. This adds cost and limits Thailand's role as a direct feedstock source for premium European routes.

## Import / Trade / Local-Preference Considerations
- Thailand is largely self-sufficient in ethanol and biodiesel for domestic mandate compliance
- Some palm oil is imported from Malaysia and Indonesia for biodiesel production
- No formal import restrictions on biofuels, but the domestic mandate creates strong local offtake
- Thailand is a potential ethanol exporter when domestic production exceeds mandate requirements, but this is not a primary market dynamic

### In plain English
Thailand does not need imported biofuel. It produces its own. The commercial relevance is understanding Thai production costs as a benchmark, not selling into Thailand.

## Temporal Rules and Transition Windows
- AEDP 2018-2037 provides the current policy framework
- AEDP 2024 update under development — may revise targets and blending ratios
- B20 operational treatment could increase domestic biodiesel demand
- No formal sunset or transition windows for existing mandates

### What experts should watch
- AEDP 2024 update (could change ethanol and biodiesel targets)
- B20 operational details and AEDP update (would affect palm oil demand and regional feedstock availability)
- sugarcane and cassava crop yields (affect ethanol production economics and regional pricing)
- Thai ethanol export behavior when domestic production outpaces mandate requirements

## Implementation and Administrative Friction
For a team not targeting Thailand as a destination market, the main frictions are informational:
- partial English-language sources (EPPO provides some English documentation, but primary legislation is in Thai)
- agricultural commodity price volatility affecting production economics
- limited transparency in ethanol and biodiesel producer margins
- no sustainability certification infrastructure aligned with European requirements

### What this means for mixed teams
Thailand is not a friction-heavy market for exporters because it is not a target market. The friction is in benchmarking — getting reliable data on Thai production costs and understanding how Thai supply affects regional pricing.

## Supply-Side Context
Thailand is Southeast Asia's largest ethanol producer. Key supply-side dynamics:
- sugarcane is the primary ethanol feedstock (seasonal, crop-yield dependent)
- cassava is a significant secondary feedstock (more price-volatile than sugarcane)
- palm oil is imported from Malaysia and Indonesia for biodiesel production
- domestic production capacity can exceed mandate requirements in good crop years, creating occasional export surplus

For a team building ethanol routes, Thailand sets the regional cost floor. Thai sugarcane-ethanol economics determine what price premium a waste-based or advanced ethanol route needs to justify itself.

## Southeast Asia Relevance (Expanded)
Thailand is relevant to Southeast Asia strategy in three ways:

1. **Ethanol benchmarking**: Thai sugarcane and cassava ethanol production costs set the regional baseline. Any Southeast Asia ethanol strategy must be compared against Thai economics.
2. **Feedstock competition**: Thai sugarcane and cassava demand affects regional agricultural commodity pricing, which can indirectly influence waste-stream availability.
3. **Policy direction signal**: Thailand's AEDP updates signal how Southeast Asian governments think about biofuel scaling — useful for anticipating where the region moves next.

### Regional interaction
- Thailand imports palm oil from Malaysia and Indonesia for biodiesel — creating cross-border supply chain dependencies.
- Thai ethanol surplus (in good crop years) can enter regional markets, affecting pricing.
- Singapore and other smaller ASEAN markets often look to Thailand as a policy and pricing reference.

## Value-Capture Mechanics
Thailand does not have a compliance-value market. Value capture is straightforward:
- ethanol is sold at or near standard fuel pricing to meet mandate requirements
- there is no premium mechanism for low-GHG or waste-based pathways
- value is driven by agricultural commodity economics — sugarcane and cassava prices, crop yields, and processing efficiency

### What this means in practice
Thai biofuel value is agricultural-commodity value, not compliance-value. The market does not reward environmental differentiation. This is fundamentally different from European systems where GHG performance creates premium pricing.

## Priority Legal Asymmetries for Cross-Market Comparison

### Thailand vs Malaysia
- Thailand has ethanol mandates; Malaysia does not (palm biodiesel only).
- Thailand uses sugarcane and cassava for ethanol; Malaysia uses palm oil for biodiesel.
- Thailand is more relevant for ethanol benchmarking; Malaysia is more relevant for palm-derived waste feedstocks.
- Both are volumetric mandate systems with no GHG-value mechanism.

### Thailand vs Indonesia
- Thailand is the regional ethanol leader; Indonesia has no significant ethanol market.
- Both have biodiesel mandates, but Thailand's is smaller and palm-dependent (imported palm).
- Indonesia's B40 mandate absorbs more palm supply domestically than Thailand's biodiesel program.

### Thailand vs European markets
- Thailand is purely volumetric — no compliance-value premium.
- European markets create premium value for waste-based and low-GHG pathways.
- Thai conventional ethanol cannot compete in European premium markets without a fundamental pathway shift (waste-based feedstock, certification, GHG improvement).
- The gap between Thai domestic pricing and European premium pricing defines the opportunity for any route bridging the two.

### Thailand vs Brazil
- Both are major sugarcane-ethanol producers.
- Brazil has a more mature ethanol market with flex-fuel vehicle infrastructure.
- Thailand's ethanol market is mandate-driven; Brazil's is partly mandate-driven and partly consumer-driven.
- For European premium-market targeting, Brazilian and Thai conventional ethanol face similar constraints.

## Commercial Decision Criteria
Treat Thailand as a benchmarking and competitive-context market when:
- the team needs to understand regional ethanol cost positioning,
- the strategy involves Southeast Asian feedstock sourcing and needs to account for Thai agricultural demand,
- or the team is assessing whether waste-based or advanced ethanol routes can compete with Thai conventional supply.

Do not treat Thailand as a primary destination market or as a direct feedstock source for European premium routes unless:
- a specific waste-based Thai feedstock stream becomes available with proper certification,
- or Thai ethanol surplus pricing creates a rare arbitrage window.

## What Would Change This Conclusion
- AEDP 2024 update introducing GHG-performance incentives (would change Thailand from purely volumetric to partially value-driven)
- formal sustainability certification requirements in Thai biofuel policy (would align Thai supply with European demand)
- B20 expansion or operational strengthening (tightens domestic palm demand, affects regional feedstock availability)
- major Thai ethanol export expansion (could create new supply routes or competitive dynamics)
- regional trade agreement changes affecting biofuel tariff treatment

## Worked Example: Using Thai Ethanol as a Competitive Benchmark
**Scenario:** A team is evaluating whether a waste-based ethanol route from Southeast Asia can compete in the European market.

1. The team needs a cost benchmark. Thai sugarcane ethanol is the natural reference point — it is the largest, most established ethanol production in the region.
2. Thai sugarcane ethanol production cost (including feedstock, processing, and logistics to port) sets the regional floor price for conventional ethanol.
3. A waste-based route (e.g., cellulosic ethanol from agricultural residues) must either:
   - achieve a lower production cost than Thai conventional ethanol, or
   - earn a sufficient GHG-performance premium in the European destination market to justify the higher cost.
4. In European markets like Germany, the waste-based route can earn a compliance-value premium that Thai conventional ethanol cannot access.
5. The commercial question becomes: does the European premium for waste-based ethanol exceed the cost difference between the waste-based route and Thai conventional ethanol?

### What this reveals
Thailand is not the market — it is the benchmark. The team uses Thai economics to determine whether the premium-route investment is justified.

## What Non-Experts Usually Miss
- Thailand is not a "competitor" in the sense of competing for the same European buyers. It is a benchmark that determines what cost position a premium route needs to beat.
- Thai ethanol economics are agricultural-commodity driven, not compliance-value driven — a fundamentally different value logic.
- Thailand's importance is greatest for ethanol strategies; for biodiesel strategies, Malaysia and Indonesia matter more.
- English-language regulatory sources from Thailand are partial — the team should not expect the same accessibility as German or Dutch sources.
